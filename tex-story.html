Chapter One

The God
Awful
Noise.

Brad is at work. An 18-wheeler pulls up to the job site. The driver gets out, looking like a man who has had a longer day than he planned.

He mentions the noise. Some kind of sound from under the truck — been going on for a while, he says. He doesn't know what it is. He's been hoping it would stop.

Brad looks.

A gray tabby kitten. Cross-eyed. Small. Absolutely furious about the situation. Wedged up in the undercarriage of a truck that had traveled who knows how many miles from somewhere in Texas — the driver genuinely couldn't say where he'd picked him up.

Nobody knows how long Tex had been under that truck. Nobody knows what exit he boarded at. Nobody knows what, exactly, his plan was — if he had one. What is known: he was here now, in Georgia, and he had opinions about it, and he was expressing those opinions at full volume.

Brad called home. "You're not going to believe this."

She believed it immediately.

"The driver didn't know where he'd come from. The kitten wasn't saying. The only thing anybody knew for certain was that he was cross-eyed, gray, and extremely loud about his opinions."

Day one. Somewhere in Georgia. Previously: somewhere in Texas.
Immediately comfortable. Nobody asked him to be.
Chapter Two

Terrified First.
Then.

He came home terrified. That part was normal — he'd just ridden under a truck for hundreds of miles and been extracted by a stranger. Fear made complete sense.

He hid. He was small. He was quiet in the careful, watching way of an animal that hasn't decided yet whether this place is safe.

The existing residents were introduced. Gizmo — large, warm, Brad's, eighteen pounds of opinion — looked at this new cross-eyed arrival and formed a position. That position has not changed. We will get to that.

Spitz, though. Spitz — the soul cat, the original, the fifteen-year resident who hissed from a Frito-Lay box and chose his person by morning — looked at this tiny, terrified, cross-eyed fugitive from Texas and made a different kind of decision. He claimed him. Not loudly. Not with ceremony. Just the slow, deliberate way Spitz does everything — showing up next to him, staying there, making it known that this one was under his jurisdiction now.

The cat who arrived hissing in a Frito-Lay box adopted the cat who arrived screaming under an 18-wheeler. Of course he did. He recognized something.

And then, slowly — over days — the fear gave way to something else entirely.

The terrified kitten from under the 18-wheeler became a gray tabby who would launch himself off furniture at 2am for no reason, stare at corners of rooms where nothing existed, and fall completely, immediately, unconditionally in love with whoever happened to be nearby — right up until he didn't.

The crazy revealed itself gradually. Like a sunrise. Like a road trip where you don't realize how far you've gone until you look up.

They named him Tex. It was the only honest option.

The calm before the chaos. It did not last long.
🤠 Known Profile — Tex, aka The Cross-Eyed Fugitive

Eyes: Both pointed dramatically inward. He sees everything in triplicate. Chipmunks, squirrels, shadows, furniture — all of it: three of each. At all times.

Disposition: Purring, loving, butter-soft. Then — without warning, without provocation, without any detectable change in atmospheric pressure — a low growl, pinned ears, and the specific energy of a cat who has decided your face needs to be removed. Then back to purring.

Known Hazards: The balcony rails. He lays on them. He stands on them. He perches on ledge edges overlooking two-story drops with the calm confidence of someone who has done the math and arrived at a different answer than everyone else in the room.

Origin: Somewhere in Texas. Unknown. The driver didn't know. Tex isn't saying. 🤠

Chapter Three

The Balcony
Situation.

He sees three of everything. He has placed himself on every ledge in the house. These facts are related.

There is a balcony railing in this house. Iron balusters, wood rail, two-story drop to hardwood floors below. It is not a cat perch. It was not designed as one. Tex does not care about this.

He lays on the rail like a deployment — belly draped over the wood, legs hanging on either side, completely at peace with gravity and with the two-story nothing below him. He sits on the ledge post at the top of the stairs and stares at the chandelier with the serene confidence of a cat who has evaluated the jump and found it acceptable.

We have photographed this. Repeatedly. Because at some point you stop trying to stop him and you start documenting it for the people who won't believe you.

He sees three of everything. A squirrel at the window isn't a squirrel. It's three squirrels, stacked on top of each other, conspiring. A chipmunk in the yard is a chipmunk convention. The shadow on the wall at 11pm is a full committee.

He stares at corners. Empty corners. He will sit in front of a wall and stare at it with the specific concentration of a cat who is receiving information that we are not. Whatever is over there — we cannot see it. He can see three of it. He is monitoring the situation.

We have made peace with the balcony. The balcony has not made peace with him. This is fine. He doesn't need it to.

Chapter Four

Purring.
Then Demon.

Here is a thing Tex does. He will come find you. Climb onto you, or next to you, and begin purring — the deep, rolling kind that means everything is right in the world. He is warm. He is soft. He is a cross-eyed gray tabby who made a long journey and found his people and is happy about it.

You will begin to relax. You will begin to think: he has settled. He is calm. He is fine.

And then — without warning, without reason, without any sign that you could have seen coming even if you'd been watching for it — the purring stops. The ears go flat. A low sound begins, from somewhere deep in his chest. He looks at you. Both eyes pointed in slightly different directions. The look says: I have just remembered something about you, and it is not good.

This is the moment you move. Slowly. Without sudden gestures.

And then — just as fast — it's over. He is purring again. He is soft again. He is laying on your feet and acting like nothing happened, because for him, nothing did.

He is not aggressive. He is not mean. He is simply operating on information the rest of us don't have access to, making decisions based on a threat assessment that exists entirely within his own cross-eyed, triple-visioned, formerly-under-an-18-wheeler reality.

We have come to respect this. You have to.

"He'll be purring in your lap and then he looks at you like you've wronged him personally. You haven't. But you will apologize anyway. That's just how Tex works."

Currently purring. Status could change. Stay alert.
Spitz and Tex. The original adopted the fugitive. Of course he did.
Chapter Five

Gizmo
Wants
Violence.

There is no resolution to the Gizmo situation. There has never been a resolution to the Gizmo situation. There will not be one.

Gizmo — bottle baby, baseball field, Brad's floof, eighteen pounds of warm tabby who has lived in this house since before Tex existed — looked at this cross-eyed gray fugitive from Texas and formed an opinion. That opinion is: no. Hard no. Categorical, enduring, non-negotiable no.

They do not share rooms. This is not a boundary that was negotiated. It is a law of physics. Gizmo in one room means Tex in a different room. The house operates in distinct zones. Everyone understands the zones. The zones hold.

But here is the thing about Tex: he knows. Somehow, through walls, through floors, through whatever paranormal frequency his cross-eyed triple vision runs on — he always knows when Gizmo is coming.

And he announces it.

Not with a hiss. Not with a growl. With a full-volume, sustained, from-the-depths-of-his-soul cat scream that fills every room in the house simultaneously. A sound that makes guests freeze mid-sentence. A sound that has caused at least one visitor to ask if someone was being murdered. A sound that communicates, with complete clarity: Gizmo. Adjacent. Unacceptable. Everyone should know about this.

Gizmo, for the record, walks past with the expression of a cat who has already won and knows it.

Tex screams louder.

This is Tuesday.

"If Tex even suspects Gizmo is coming, the whole house knows about it. It sounds like someone is being murdered. Nobody is being murdered. This is just communication."

Gizmo. The reason for the screaming. Completely unmoved by this.

Tex Now

Still Cross-Eyed.
Still On The Rail. Still Here.

He is six years old now and he has not calmed down and we have not asked him to. He still lays on the balcony rail like a man who finds nothing unusual about his position relative to the floor below. He still sits on the ledge post and contemplates the chandelier with the patience of someone who has time. He still stares at the corner of the hallway at 11pm — whatever is over there, he has been monitoring it for six years and he is not finished.

He still purrs. He still, without warning, stops. He still looks at you with both eyes pointed in slightly different directions and the expression of a cat who has received new information about your character. You have learned not to take it personally. It passes. It always passes.

He belongs to no one person. He belongs to the house. He belongs to the rail and the ledge and the corners of rooms where nothing is visible to the human eye. He chose this place — or the truck chose it for him, which may be the same thing — and he has never reconsidered it.

Somewhere in Texas, a decision was made. We don't know when. We don't know where. The driver didn't know. Tex isn't saying. What we know is that the god awful noise under that truck was him, announcing himself, from hundreds of miles out.

He has been announcing himself ever since. Cross-eyed, gray-striped, perched on something he shouldn't be. Exactly as he arrived. Exactly as he intends to remain. 🤠
