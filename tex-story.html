His Story

"Brad calls me from work. Says the driver's talking about some god awful noise under his truck. Brad looks. And there's this tiny, cross-eyed gray kitten — just there. Somewhere in Texas he had made a decision. Nobody consulted us. We would have done anything for him. We did everything we could. It was never enough time."

Chapter One

The God
Awful
Noise.

Brad is at work. An 18-wheeler pulls up to the job site. The driver gets out looking like a man who has had a longer day than he planned for.

He mentions the noise. Some kind of sound from under the truck — been going on a while, he says. Started somewhere in Texas. He's been hoping it would stop.

Brad looks.

A gray tabby kitten. Cross-eyed. Small. Absolutely furious about the situation. Wedged up in the undercarriage of a truck that had driven who-knows-how-many miles across state lines. The driver genuinely couldn't say where he'd picked him up. He just knew the noise started somewhere in Texas and hadn't stopped since.

Nobody knows how long Tex was under that truck. Nobody knows what exit he boarded at. Nobody knows what his plan was — if he had one. What is known: he was cross-eyed, he was gray, he was small, and he had opinions.

Big, loud, sustained opinions about all of this.

Brad called home. "You're not going to believe this."

She believed it immediately. She always does.

"The driver didn't know where he'd come from. Tex wasn't saying. The only things anyone knew for certain: cross-eyed, gray, and extremely loud about his opinions."

The face that rode under an 18-wheeler and had zero regrets about it.
Coming to the door. His yard. His terms. Always.
Chapter Two

Purring.
Then
Demon.

He came home terrified. That part made complete sense — he'd just ridden under a truck for hundreds of miles and been extracted by a stranger. Fear was appropriate. Fear was earned.

But the fear didn't last. What came after the fear was Tex.

He would find you. Climb onto you or curl up next to you and begin purring — that deep, rolling, everything-is-right-in-the-world purr. Warm. Soft. Completely at peace. You would begin to relax. You would think: he has settled. He is calm. He is fine.

And then — without warning, without reason, without a single atmospheric change you could have detected even if you'd been watching for it — the purring would stop. The ears would go flat. A low sound would start from somewhere deep in his chest. Both eyes pointed in slightly different directions, looking at you like you'd personally wronged him in a past life.

That was the moment you moved. Slowly. No sudden gestures.

And then — just as fast — it was over. Purring again. Soft again. Laying on your feet like nothing had happened, because for him, nothing had.

He was not mean. He was operating on information the rest of us didn't have access to. Making decisions based on a threat assessment that existed entirely within his own cross-eyed, triple-visioned, formerly-under-an-18-wheeler reality. We respected this. You had to.

Chapter Three

He Sees
Three Of
Everything.

Both eyes pointed slightly inward. Whatever was in that corner — he could see three of it. We could see none of it. He was monitoring the situation.

The cross-eyes were not a flaw. They were a feature. They gave him a view of the world that no other living creature in this household had access to — a world where there were three chipmunks at every window, three squirrels in every yard, three suspicious shadows on every wall at 11pm.

He stared at corners. Empty corners. He would sit in front of a blank wall with the concentration of a cat receiving information that we simply were not cleared to hear. Whatever was over there — he had it handled. He'd been handling it for seven years. We never found out what it was.

Outside was three of everything too. He would stand in the yard and survey the fence line with the bearing of a general who had assessed the terrain and found it satisfactory. Nose up. Tail straight. Plaid collar catching the light. Cross-eyes scanning three different angles simultaneously.

No chipmunk could outmaneuver him. They might think they could — but they were only one chipmunk. He was tracking three of them. At all times.

It was, objectively, one of the most spectacular ways to be a cat.

Perimeter check. Last one. Done right.
He always knew where he was going. Even when nobody else did.

Chapter Four

I Would Have
Given
Anything.

Here is the part that isn't funny. The part that lives underneath all the laughing.

She would have given anything. She would have given everything. Every vet bill, every specialist, every phone call at 6am, every middle-of-the-night worry — she paid all of it without hesitation and would have paid it ten times over if it meant more time with this ridiculous, cross-eyed, plaid-collared chaos agent from Texas.

The chronic pain was not something she could fix. Not for lack of trying. Not for lack of love. Not for lack of trying absolutely everything there was to try. Some things you cannot fix with enough love, and that is the cruelest fact about loving an animal this hard.

She gave him a backyard walkabout on his last morning. Let him wander like he was on a great expedition — nose up, plaid collar in the sun, checking the fence line one more time, sniffing things that probably only he could smell. She followed him from a careful distance with her phone, photographing everything, wanting every last second.

And then she held him while he went to sleep, so he would not be alone and scared. That is the whole measure of a person. That is who she was to him. She was the one who held him at the end. He was not alone. He was not scared. He was loved so completely that it fills every room she walks into now with the specific silence of someone who should still be there.

She would have given anything. She did give everything. That was always going to be the whole story.

"He was so stressed at the vet they had to sedate him. But I was allowed to hold him while he went to sleep. I would have done anything to make him comfortable and not chronically hurting. It's so unfair."

Last walkabout. April 8, 2026. He did it properly.
Always one more thing to investigate. Always.

🤠 In Loving Memory

He Was Cross-Eyed,
Plaid-Collared,
And Completely Irreplaceable.

Sitting in the sun. Doing his rounds.
The plaid collar. Always plaid.
The last walkabout. Done right.

He came from Texas. We don't know where exactly. The driver didn't know. Tex wasn't saying. But he ended up here — in a house full of bow ties and rhinestone collars and chaos and love — wearing a plaid collar that was more honest than anything else in the room.

He was cross-eyed and he saw three of everything and he monitored corners that held nothing detectable to the human eye and he could hear Gizmo coming through walls by some kind of cross-eyed sonar that defied explanation and he announced it every single time at full volume and Gizmo was never, not once, intimidated by this. That is fine. Tex was right to try. He was always right to try.

He was bipolar in the specific way of a cat who had access to information we didn't — some threat assessment that ran quietly in the background of every purring moment, that could flip without notice and return just as fast, leaving you slightly shaken and him completely unbothered. You learned not to take it personally. It was never personal. It was just Tex.

He was chronically hurting at the end. That is the part that she would have given anything to fix. The part that wasn't fixable with more love, more vet visits, more trying. She tried everything. She gave everything. And when trying everything was no longer enough, she gave him the only thing she had left to give: a backyard walkabout in the April sun and her arms around him at the end so he wasn't alone.

He was not alone. He was held by someone who loved him so completely that the house still knows he's gone.

"Somewhere in Texas, a cross-eyed gray kitten made a decision.
He rode under an 18-wheeler all the way to us.
It was the best bad decision anyone ever made.
We are so glad he made it. We are so glad he was ours.
We would have done anything for more time.
🤠 Good boy, Tex. Good, chaotic, irreplaceable, plaid-collared boy."

He Didn't Ask For Permission.
He Never Needed To.

Every rescue starts somewhere. Sometimes it's a Ring camera at 2am and a feral mother. Sometimes it's a creek and a Frito-Lay box. Sometimes it's a baseball field where a man is umpiring and a bottle baby needs holding. And sometimes — sometimes it's an 18-wheeler, a god awful noise, and a man who called home and said: you're not going to believe this. She believed it immediately. She always does. That's why this house exists. That's why these stories get told. In memory of Tex, and in honor of every animal still waiting for someone to look under the truck.
