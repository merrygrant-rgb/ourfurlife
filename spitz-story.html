Chapter One

She Almost
Walked Past.
She Didn't.

She was working late. It was drizzling. She was walking to her car in the dark when she heard something near the creek that ran along the front of her building.

She stopped. She stood there. And she heard it again.

A kitten. Alone. At the base of a tree. The creek had probably risen — she suspected his mother had been moving a litter when something went wrong. He was cold. He was small. He was furious about all of it.

She went back into the warehouse. Found rags. Found an empty Frito-Lay chips box. Came back out. Picked him up — and he let her know, in no uncertain terms, exactly how he felt about being picked up.

He hissed all the way to the car. He hissed for the entire drive home in the rain. Solid. Hour. Of. Hissing.

She didn't put him back.

"She heard something she almost didn't stop for. She stopped. That's the whole story."

The beginning. A Frito-Lay box. A drizzly night. A choice.
Smaller than a TV remote. Already enormous in personality.
Chapter Two

Welding Gloves.
A Crate.
Hi Spitz.

They got him home. They needed welding gloves just to get him into the crate. He spit at her oldest daughter through the wire door — repeatedly, enthusiastically, without a single apology.

He was named in that moment. Not with deliberation. Not with a list. Just the immediate, obvious, completely accurate truth of what he was doing.

Hi Spitz.

They put in a heating pad. They put in some gruel. They said goodnight to a hissing, spitting, furious tiny cat who wanted absolutely nothing to do with any of them.

The next morning — warmth. Food. And then someone brought out a bottle.

He stopped hissing. Instantly. The feral became a furbaby in the space of one bottle feeding, one warm morning, one moment of realizing that hands meant safety and voices meant food and this place — whatever it was — was not the cold creek anymore.

He chose her pillow that morning. He has chosen it every night for fifteen years.

Brad said absolutely not. Spitz disagreed. Spitz won.
Chapter Three

Brad Came Home
To A Hissing Box.

They already had two dogs and a cat. Spitz was not interested in the headcount.

She brought home a Frito-Lay box with a hissing kitten inside. Brad wasn't thrilled — they already had two cats and this one had spent the entire car ride at maximum volume communicating his feelings about the situation.

But Spitz had his own plan. He didn't need anyone to engineer a moment or run a strategy. He simply — once he settled in, once the warmth and the bottle and the pillow became real — decided who he belonged to.

He decided on her. He has never reconsidered.

The tiny kitten who arrived in a Frito-Lay box grew into a cat who has slept on her pillow for fifteen years, who watches her with the particular devotion of an animal who decided early and never wavered. He is hers. She is his. It was settled by morning after that first bottle and nothing since has changed it.

That's just how Spitz does things. Quietly. Completely. On his own terms and nobody else's.

Brad's shoulder. Night one. The argument was already over.
He chose this house. He has never reconsidered.
Chapter Four

Thirty Days.
Miles Walked.
Dad's Birthday.

In 2016, Spitz vanished. To this day, nobody knows how he got outside. He was indoor only. And then he was gone.

She walked miles looking for him. She learned later that indoor cats stay close to home — but she didn't know that yet, so she walked. She baited a neighbor's yard with salmon. She set traps. She caught possums. She caught raccoons. She caught one very angry unrelated cat who had absolutely nothing to say to her.

No Spitz.

For thirty days. Thirty days. She was a disaster. Brad didn't know what to do with her. The house felt wrong in a way that's hard to describe — the absence of someone who has chosen your pillow every single night is its own kind of silence.

And then they were coming home one night — day thirty — and she saw him. Standing in the neighbor's front yard. Just standing there, like he'd been thinking about coming back for a while and had finally made up his mind.

He saw them. He ran. Of course he ran.

The face that kept her walking for thirty days.
Safe. Home. Acting like nothing happened.
🌹 Operation Rose Bush — Classified

He ran across the street. She knew he'd try to cross again. She was convinced he'd be hit by a car. There was only one option.

Climb into the neighbor's rose bushes. At night. Without asking permission. Without thinking about snakes. Only Spitz.

He was mewing at her but looking around like he was going to dart. She went in anyway. Belly crawling. Through rose bushes. In the dark. For a cat who had been gone for thirty days and was currently reconsidering his options.

She lunged. She secured him.

Brad pulled her out by her legs.

They found him on her late father's birthday.

She believes her Dad had a hand in it. So do we. 💙

Brad — Rose Bush Extraction Services, Unpaid
Chapter Five

Save Him.
Two Words.

In 2022, Spitz stopped eating. The vet said the word nobody wants to hear — cancer was possible. She was a disaster. Brad didn't know what to do with her. The house went quiet in that specific, terrible way it goes when something you love is in danger.

Brad is the most frugal man she has ever known, and she loves him for it. He thinks carefully about money. He is measured and practical and deliberate.

He handed the vet $2,500 and said two words:

"Save him."

They put in an esophageal feeding tube. She moved to the couch. She set alarms every four hours. For weeks, she tube-fed her soul cat through the night, every night, without complaint, because there was no version of this story where she didn't.

"If I ever questioned how much I was loved — I knew in that moment I was cherished."

The man who said absolutely not to a Frito-Lay box kitten fifteen years ago spent $2,500 without blinking to save him. For her. Because she is his and Spitz is hers and that's how this works.

The feeding tube. The couch. Every four hours. She didn't miss once.
Six months later. Healed. Unbothered. Cowboy hat pending.

Spitz Now

Pillow. BedJet Vent.
Still Hers. Always.

He is older now — fifteen years of mornings on the same pillow, fifteen years of choosing the same person, fifteen years of being exactly where he decided to be that first warm morning after the bottle. He sleeps with his head on her pillow, his body stretched along hers — or, if he gets chilly, at the foot of the bed on the BedJet vent, because he has standards and the BedJet is warm and nobody asked him to explain himself.

There are cameras throughout the house now so she can drop in and talk to him when she travels. Her daughters stay on watch. He is monitored, loved, and occasionally appearing on video calls from across the country because he needs to see her face and she needs to see his.

He was the first. The original. Every rescue that came after — Dallas, Gizmo, Nova, all of them — they exist because fifteen years ago she stopped by a creek in the drizzle and listened for a sound she almost missed.

He started everything. And he is still here, on her pillow, proof that the right decision and the right cat can change an entire life.

He is her soul cat. She will be destroyed when he goes. She knows this. She chooses it every single day. That's what love is.
